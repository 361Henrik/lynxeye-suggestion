# Simplification Brief: Lynxeye Executive Version

## Purpose

This duplicated project is the simplified executive-facing version of the Lynxeye proposal app.

It is not a refactor of the current flagship version. It is a parallel sales instrument built from the same strategic intelligence, but with a shorter decision path, lower cognitive load, and clearer commercial framing.

The flagship version explains the full thinking. This version should help Christian, Niklas, Johan, Viking, and the partner team quickly understand what the project does, why it matters now, and how it can become practical value inside Lynxeye.

## Governing Frame

Help Lynxeye employees turn incoming AI infrastructure into measurable client value and company results.

Headquarters can provide infrastructure, approved tools, security, data access, platforms, and guardrails.

Lynxeye creates value when its people use that infrastructure in daily work, client delivery, pitch work, and reusable company practice.

Henrik’s role is the practical bridge between available AI infrastructure and working adoption inside Lynxeye.

## What Changed After The Client Conversations

The simplified version should reflect the May 18 and May 19 conversations with Christian.

Key shifts:

- Eraneos / headquarters is bringing more AI infrastructure, tools, and group-level direction.
- Lynxeye should not position itself only as a passive recipient of that infrastructure.
- The stronger opportunity is for Lynxeye to become a fast adopter and potential showcase inside the wider group.
- Christian is concerned about cost defensibility.
- The project must show enough value spread to justify Henrik’s involvement, without making the pilot too wide or slow.
- People do not yet know what they do not know. The proposal must leave room for discovery.
- Success must be concrete enough to defend investment, but not so rigid that it blocks learning.
- Employee confidence, skepticism, discomfort, and uneven AI maturity are real adoption factors.
- Compliance, privacy, client IP, approved tool use, and quality review must be visible.
- The simulator should remain directional and adjustable, not presented as proof.

## Retired Framing

Do not use “Escape Velocity” as the main project frame in the simplified version.

Use clearer language:

- Part A: Align & Scope
- Part B: Working Pilot
- Later: Scale Decision

The project is two paid parts, with a later scale decision after evidence exists.

## New Project Logic

### Part A: Align & Scope

A small paid scope sprint.

Purpose: reduce uncertainty before the main pilot.

Part A should validate:

- the first 3 to 5 priority use cases
- the relevant HQ infrastructure and tool direction
- the right pilot participants
- the live work or pitch context
- adoption risks
- compliance and IP boundaries
- what success should look like
- what Part B should include
- the price and scope logic for Part B

Part A is complete and useful on its own. It is not free pre-work.

### Part B: Working Pilot

The main engagement.

Purpose: turn the scoped opportunity into working practice.

Part B includes:

- hands-on setup
- tool, connector, and workflow configuration
- live client work or pitch work
- coaching and show-and-tell
- prototype builds where useful
- playbook capture
- quality and compliance routines
- simulator calibration
- success evidence
- scale decision package

Part B should be flexible inside agreed boundaries, because the team will learn what matters as they experience the work.

### Later: Scale Decision

Not priced now.

The scale decision follows evidence from Part A and Part B.

Possible directions:

- more people
- more teams
- more use cases
- deeper playbook capture
- stronger governance rhythm
- wider adoption program
- more automation or prototype development

## Core Matrix

The simplified version should use a 3 by 3 matrix as the main mental model.

The matrix is not just a table. It is the navigation model for the whole proposal.

Columns:

1. Setup & daily use
2. Live client work
3. Pitches & growth

Rows:

1. Individual employee
2. Team / practice
3. Lynxeye company

The first view should be calm and high-level. Detailed activities should appear through tabs or supporting panels, not inside every first-view matrix cell.

## Matrix Content

| Level               | Setup & daily use                                         | Live client work                                                  | Pitches & growth                                                            |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Individual employee | Personal AI readiness, tools, context, confidence, habits | Coached use of AI inside actual work                              | Faster contribution to pitch thinking, structure, synthesis, and production |
| Team / practice     | Shared setup patterns and review routines                 | 3 to 5 priority use cases tested through real work and prototypes | Better pitch speed, quality, and output formats                             |
| Lynxeye company     | Setup guide for wider adoption                            | Playbooks, guardrails, and reusable methods                       | Refined value model and scale decision package                              |

## Matrix Tabs

Use tabs to change the lens on the matrix:

- Overview: what each cell means at a high level.
- Part A: what should be validated in each cell.
- Part B: what Henrik and Lynxeye will do in each cell.
- Adoption & Guardrails: what must be trusted, safe, human-led, and compliant.
- Success & Evidence: what Lynxeye should see, feel, and measure.

This is preferable to presenting the old 13 deliverables as the first-read structure.

## Cross-Cutting Rails

These are not separate columns. They apply across the entire matrix.

### Human Confidence & Adoption

Some people will move fast. Others will need proof, repetition, and a safer way in.

The project should not frame this as resistance. It is adoption reality.

The work should help employees see that AI is not replacing human judgment. It extends, structures, and accelerates human work when used well.

### Compliance, IP & Quality

The goal is not random tool use.

The simplified version should show that approved tools, privacy boundaries, client IP protection, data handling, and human review are part of the working method from the start.

HQ may provide the infrastructure and rules, but Lynxeye still needs practical daily habits that follow them.

### Practice, See, Adjust, Improve

The adoption model is practical:

Show it. Try it. Adjust it. Use it again. Capture what works. Improve the method through the next case.

This is how employees move from ad hoc AI chat to structured orchestration.

## Placement Of Matrix And Simulator

The matrix should come before the simulator.

Reason:

- The matrix explains where value happens.
- The simulator then explains why that value may matter commercially.
- If the simulator appears too early, the page risks feeling like math before method.

Recommended order:

1. Promise
2. HQ infrastructure vs Lynxeye working value
3. Matrix
4. Part A / Part B
5. Simulator
6. Working method
7. Success and scale decision

## What To Keep From The Flagship

Keep, but simplify:

- simulator
- ping-pong vs orchestration
- live-case loop
- personal AI setup
- use-case map
- playbook capture
- quality guardrails
- Henrik’s hands-on working style
- first-person warmth where Henrik is speaking
- the idea that human judgment leads

## What To Reduce Or Remove

Reduce or remove:

- the 13 deliverables as the first-read structure
- the 6 add-ons as a shopping list
- heavy phase naming
- long explanatory sections
- abstract AI transformation language
- too many conceptual jumps before the practical offer is clear
- broad team expansion before Part A is complete

The current deliverables are still useful source material, but they should be reorganized into the matrix and Part A / Part B logic.

## Treatment Of Current Deliverables

Do not discard the old deliverables. Re-map them.

Suggested mapping:

- AI ambition and operating principles: Part A, company level, setup & daily use.
- Current AI practice working session: Part A, team/company level, setup & daily use.
- Pilot team selection: Part A, individual/team level, all columns.
- Engagement charter: Part A, company level, adoption and guardrails.
- Personal AI setup: Part B, individual level, setup & daily use.
- Documented setup guide: Part B, company level, setup & daily use.
- Live-case selection: Part A into Part B, team level, live client work.
- Live-case workflow design and coaching: Part B, team level, live client work.
- First Lynxeye playbooks: Part B, company level, live client work.
- Light quality guardrails and review rhythms: Part A and Part B, cross-cutting rail.
- Use-case map and opportunity surface: Part A starts it, Part B validates it.
- Refined simulator: Part B, company level, pitches & growth / value logic.
- Phase 2 recommendation document: rename as scale decision package.

## Add-Ons Reframed

Do not present add-ons as a shopping list in the first read.

Treat them as possible expansion paths after Part A or Part B:

- deeper playbook capture
- more teams
- wider adoption rhythm
- internal showcase or feedback loop
- extended coaching cadence
- stronger governance model

The user should feel that the project has a clear starting path, not a menu that must be decoded before they understand the offer.

## Pricing And Commercial Logic

Do not price Phase 3.

Part A should be a smaller paid scope sprint.

Part B can be priced after Part A validates scope, use cases, team shape, and success criteria.

The simulator can include adjustable project cost logic so Christian can test value defensibility, but all numbers must remain directional and clearly caveated.

The commercial argument should be:

- not all freed time becomes value
- adoption quality determines value capture
- better pitches, faster delivery, and reusable playbooks create value together
- Part A exists to reduce pricing and scope uncertainty before Part B
