Captured capacity

Formula: employees x billable hours x delivery compression x capture rate, shown with uncertainty. Directional only. 10,900 to 14,800 hours For Lynxeye at 70 people, roughly 12,850 hours survives work creep before uncertainty.

Gross capacity value

Formula: captured capacity range x blended billing rate. This is gross equivalent value, not net profit. 32.6 to 44.3M SEK If captured as billable work at 3,000 SEK/hr.

Pitch-equivalent capacity

Formula: baseline pitches divided by compressed cycle, then minus baseline pitches. Capacity only, not a sales forecast. +3.0 At 12 baseline pitches and 20% compression.

Hours returned / consultant / week

Formula: captured capacity divided by employees and working weeks. This is what the model feels like in the week. 4.0 hours Roughly 0.5 workdays per consultant per week.

Expected additional wins

Formula: baseline pitches x baseline win rate x win-rate improvement. No revenue value is assigned. +0.4 At 12 qualified pitches per year and a 35% baseline win rate.

Captured vs absorbed time

Formula: captured time equals raw freed capacity x capture rate. Absorbed time is the remainder. 55% captured / 45% absorbed Scope & Setup and Prove In Work are partly about moving this split in the right direction.

Every 1 percentage point increase in captured time is worth roughly 210 hours / 630k SEK gross capacity at current settings.

Why orchestration

Two strategies for AI: ping-ponging, or building capability.

Most people start with ping-pong mode: ask, answer, retry, move on. It can be useful, but the value stays personal, inconsistent, and hard to scale. The organization gets activity, but not necessarily capacity.

Ping-pong with AI chat Useful, but fragile

One person tries something, gets an answer, edits it, and moves on. The next person starts again from scratch. Learning stays hidden.

Scale and orchestrate with AI Reusable working capability

The workflow is designed with context, approved tools, examples, review points, quality checks, and playbooks. Learning becomes shared practice.

This connects directly to the HQ and Eraneos setup. Group infrastructure creates the conditions. Lynxeye creates value when that infrastructure becomes smooth daily use, stronger client projects, faster and better pitches, and company-owned methods.

The work is not about replacing judgment. It is about giving Lynxeye people better ways to prepare, synthesize, produce, review, and reuse work so capacity and capability increase together.

What I have learned the hard way

I have spent endless hours ping-ponging with AI: testing models, chasing better workflows, getting frustrated by drift, loops, and almost-right answers. That is part of what I bring into this work. I know the difference between ad hoc chat that feels useful in the moment and a working system that others can actually use.

The evolution matters: from individual chat, to more structured chat, to automations, to small builds, to multimodal workflows where voice, documents, data, images, and tools start working together. The opportunity is moving fast, but speed without structure creates noise. It can make average work look polished before the thinking is good enough.

I think of today's AI as an army of very capable specialists that still need direction. They may know a lot, but they do not understand your clients, your quality bar, your politics, or your judgment unless you design the work around that. The human team still needs to ask better questions, challenge the output, and decide what matters.

My default move is to ask what perfect would look like, then reverse-engineer the practical steps from the needs of customers, clients, employees, stakeholders, and the company. Not abstract training. Not tool enthusiasm for its own sake. Real problems, better methods, clearer outcomes, and enough structure that the learning can spread.
